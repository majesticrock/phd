\begin{abstract}
  We numerically study the collective excitations present in BCS-superconductors including screened Coulomb interactions.
  By varying the screening strength, we analyze its impact on the system.
  We use a formulation of the effective phonon-mediated interaction between electrons that depends on the energy transfer between particles,
  rather than being a constant in a small energy shell around the Fermi edge.
  We compute the system's Green's functions using the iterated equations of motion (iEoM) approach,
  which ultimately enables a comprehensive analysis of collective excitations.
  For weak couplings, we identify the well-known amplitude (Higgs) mode at the quasiparticle continuum's lower edge
  and the phase (Anderson-Bogoliubov) mode at $\omega = 0$ for a neutral system, which shifts to higher energies as the Coulomb interactions are switched on.
  As the phononic coupling is increased, the Higgs mode emerges from the continuum, and additional phase and amplitude modes appear,
  persisting even with active Coulomb interactions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Model}\label{sec:model}

We will focus on a rather general Hamiltonian at zero temperature given by
\begin{equation}
\label{eqn:base_hamiltonian}
  H = H_\mathrm{kin} + H_\mathrm{Ph} + H_\mathrm{C} + H_\mathrm{BG},
\end{equation}
where $H_\mathrm{kin}$ describes the kinetic part,
$H_\mathrm{Ph}$ describes an effective electron-electron interaction mediated by phonons, 
$H_\mathrm{C}$ describes the repulsive Coulomb interaction between electrons,
and $H_\mathrm{BG}$ describes the electronic interaction between the electrons and the atomic nuclei,
which we represent as a constant positive background charge $\rho$.
This term exactly cancels with the divergent Hartree contribution in $H_\mathrm{C}$.
The individual terms are given by
\bs
\begin{align}
  H_\mathrm{Kin} &= \sum_{\vec{k} \sigma} \left( \epsilon_0 (k) - \Ef \right) n_{k\sigma} \\
  H_\mathrm{Ph} &= \frac{1}{V} \sum_{\vec{k} \vec{k'}} g(k,k') \opSCd{k} \opSC{k'} \\
  H_\mathrm{C} &= \frac{e^2}{2V \epsilon_0} \sum_{\sigma \sigma'} \sum_{\vec{k} \vec{k}' \vec{q}} \frac{1}{q^2 + k_s^2} \opd{k}{\sigma} \opd{k}{'\sigma} c_{\vec{k}'-\vec{q} \sigma'} c_{\vec{k}+\vec{q} \sigma} \\
  H_\mathrm{BG} &= - \frac{e^2}{2 \epsilon_0 k_s^2} \sum_{ \vec{k} \sigma } \rho c_{ \vec{k}, \sigma }^\dagger c_{ \vec{k}, \sigma }.
\end{align}
\es
Here, $V$ is the system's volume, $\epsilon_0 (k) = \hbar^2 k^2 / (2 m_e)$ the free particle dispersion, 
$\Ef$ the Fermi energy, $\hbar$ the reduced Planck's constant, $m_e$ the electron mass, 
$e$ its charge, $\epsilon_0$ the vacuum permittivity, and $k_s$ the Thomas-Fermi screening wavevector.
The operator $c_{ \vec{k}, \sigma }^{(\dagger)}$ annihilates (creates) an electron with momentum $\vec{k}$ and spin $\sigma$.

Obtaining an effective electron-electron interaction based on the physical electron-phonon interaction has been a major subject of past studies.
Many \textsl{ans\"atze} have been proposed that achieve just that.
Moreover, the resulting effective interactions are not necessarily identical.
One of the best-known formulations was presented by Fr\"ohlich \cite{frohlich1997}
\begin{equation}
  g^\text{Fr\"ohlich} (\vec{k},\vec{k}') = \frac{|M_{\vec{q}}|^2 \omega_{\vec{q}}}{(\epsilon(k) - \epsilon(k'))^2 - \omega_{\vec{q}}^2},
\end{equation}
where $\vec{q} \coloneqq \vec{k} - \vec{k}'$, $M_{\vec{q}}$ is the electron-phonon coupling strength,
$\omega_{\vec{q}}$ is the phonon frequency, and $\epsilon(\vec{k})$ refers to the single-particle energy.
This form has two significant disadvantages compared to other formulations, namely, that it is only attractive in a small energy interval and that it includes divergences.

Alternative \textsl{ans\"atze} resulted in expressions that remedied these issues, see, e.g., Refs. \cite{kehrein2006,lenz1996,mielke1997,mielke1997a,hubsch2003,krull2012}.
The most important commonality among all these results is that they coincide for real, i.e., energy-conserving, processes.
This agreement is essential since these processes are the ones that can be practically observed \cite{kehrein2006}.

For our purposes, we will adopt a formulation that was derived using a continuous unitary transformation (CUT) found in Ref. \cite{krull2012}
\begin{equation}
\label{eqn:phonon_int}
  g(k, k') = - \frac{|M_{\vec{q}}|^2}{\omega_{\vec{q}}} \Theta( \omega_{\vec{q}} - |\epsilon(k) - \epsilon(k')| ),
\end{equation}
where $\Theta(k)$ is the Heaviside function and the single-particle dispersion is given by $\epsilon(k) = \epsilon_0 (k) + \eFock(k) - \Ef$.
The Fock energy $\eFock (k)$ arises from the mean-field treatment of $H_\mathrm{C}$, see \eqref{eqn:fock_coulomb} below.
We will, furthermore, restrict our discussion to a single phonon mode at the Debye frequency, i.e., $\omega_{\vec{q}} = \debye$,
and a constant interaction strength $G \coloneqq |M_{\vec{q}}|^2 / \debye > 0$.
This yields the interaction term
\begin{equation}
  g(k, k') = - G \Theta( \debye - |\epsilon(k) - \epsilon(k')| ).
\end{equation}


Throughout this article, we fix the Fermi wavevector to $\hbar \kf / \sqrt{m_e} = 4.25 \sqrt{\mathrm{eV}}$.
This results in a Fermi energy around $\SI{8.5}{eV}$ to $\SI{9}{eV}$ depending on the screening wavevector $k_s$.
Additionally, we fix the Debye frequency to $\debye = \SI{10}{meV}$.
These values are taken to be close to the actual parameters found in lead and our results are qualitatively indepedent of them.

Furthermore, we define the dimensionless parameter 
\begin{equation}
  g \coloneqq \rho_\mathrm{F} G = G \frac{m_e \kf}{2 \pi^2 \hbar^2},
\end{equation}
where $\rho_\mathrm{F}$ is the density of states of the non-interacting system at the Fermi edge.

In the same manner, we define a dimensionless scaling ratio $\lambda$ for the screening wavevector
\begin{equation}
  k_s \coloneqq \lambda \sqrt{\frac{e^2 m_e}{3 \pi^2 \hbar^2 \epsilon_0} \kf}.
\end{equation}
The square-root expression is an estimate for the Thomas-Fermi wavevector in real materials provided by Ref. \cite{rickayzen1980}.
Mathematically, this screening translates to the real-space potential $V(r) \propto \exp (- k_s r) / r$.
Physically, its existence can be motivated in the following manner:
An electric field naturally causes a displacement of the free charge carriers. 
This, in turn, generates an additional electric field that displaces the electrons, generating another field and so on.
Diagramatically, this process results in an effective interaction as depicted in \autoref{fig:feynman},
which corresponds to the aforementioned screened Coulomb potential \cite{rickayzen1980}.

Lastly, we will define the following operators for brevity
\begin{equation}
  f_{\vec{k}} \coloneqq \opSC{k},\qquad n_{\vec{k}\sigma} \coloneqq \opd{k}{\sigma} \ope{k}{\sigma}.
\end{equation}

\section{The superconducting gap function}
\label{sec:mean_field}

\subsection{General considerations}

We use a mean-field approximation to decouple the interaction terms in \eqref{eqn:base_hamiltonian}.
This will grant us access to the expectation values and the gap functions of interest.

The phononic contribution boils down to a single non-vanishing term
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{H}_\mathrm{Ph} = \sum_{\vec{k}} \deltaPh (k) f_{\vec{k}}^\dagger + \mathrm{H.c.},
\end{equation}
where the phononic contribution to the superconducting gap function is given by
\begin{align}
\label{eqn:delta_phonon}
  \deltaPh (k) &= \frac{1}{V} \sum_{\vec{k'}} g(k,k')  \expec{f_k} \nonumber \\
    &= \frac{1}{2 \pi^2} \int_0^\infty \mathrm{d} k' k'^2 g(k, k') \expec{ f_k }.
\end{align}
Repeating the same calculation for $H_\mathrm{C}$ yields
\begin{align}
  \tilde{H}_\mathrm{C} &= - \sum_{\vec{k}} \deltaC (k) f_{\vec{k}}^\dagger + \mathrm{H.c.} \nonumber \\
    &+ \sum_{\vec{k} \sigma} (\eFock(k) + \epsilon_\mathrm{C}(k)) n_{\vec{k} \sigma}.
\end{align}
The additional contribution to the gap function is given by
\begin{equation}
\label{eqn:delta_coulomb}
  \deltaC (k) = \frac{e^2}{8 \pi^2 \epsilon_0 k} \int_0^\infty \mathrm{d} q \expec{f_{q}} q \ln \left( \frac{k_s^2 + (q + k)^2}{k_s^2 + (q - k)^2} \right).
\end{equation}
Note, that this term has the opposite sign of \eqref{eqn:delta_phonon}.
Assuming $\expec{n_{\vec{k} \sigma}} = \Theta (\kf - k)$, the Fock energy reads
\begin{align}
\label{eqn:fock_coulomb}
  \eFock (k) &= - \frac{e^2}{4 \pi^2 \epsilon_0} \kf \bigg[1 \nonumber \\
  &+ \frac{k_s}{\kf } \left( \arctan \left( \frac{k - \kf}{k_s} \right) - \arctan \left( \frac{k + \kf}{k_s} \right) \right) \nonumber \\
  &+ \frac{\kf^2 - k^2 + k_s^2}{2 k \kf} \ln \left( \frac{k_s^2 + (\kf + k)^2}{k_s^2 + (\kf - k)^2} \right) \bigg].
\end{align}
In the superconducting phase, however, the form of $\expec{n_{\vec{k}}}$ is more complicated.
We define the difference between these expression as $\delta n_{k} \coloneqq \Theta (\kf - k) - \expec{n_{k\sigma}}$.
Notably, this quantity is mostly close to $0$ and has a finite contribution only close to the Fermi edge.
Nevertheless, it results in a small correction to the Fock energy given by
\begin{equation}
\label{eqn:fock_corr}
  \epsilon_\mathrm{C}(k) = \frac{e^2}{8 \pi^2 \epsilon_0 k} \int_0^\infty \mathrm{d} q \delta n_{q} q \ln \left( \frac{k_s^2 + (q + k)^2}{k_s^2 + (q - k)^2} \right),
\end{equation}
which is usually of the order of a few microelectronvolts.

For our numerics, we take notice that all of the quantities above merely depend on the absolute value $k = |\vec{k}|$ due to the rotational symmetry of our model.
Therefore, we discretize $k$ using $N_k$ individual points. 
Due to the long-range nature of the Coulomb interaction,
we need to extend our discretization across a large range of $k$, theoretically even from $0$ to $\infty$,
but in practice $k \in [0, 2 \kf]$ produces good results.

Nevertheless, the main features can be observed around the Fermi edge so that an equidistant discretization would be insufficient.
To deal with this, we choose a fine mesh around the Fermi edge that utilizes $N_k / 2$ individual points and is cut off at $\kf \pm x_\mathrm{cut} \debye / \kf$.
After this cutoff, the discretization is much coarser with $N_k / 4$ additional discretization points in each direction.
A schematic representation of this can be seen in \autoref{fig:discretization}.

Choosing $x_\mathrm{cut}$ is somewhat arbitrary.
If the gap is small, a small value is beneficial as to resolve the relevant part of the gap function in more detail.
If the gap is large, however, we require a larger $x_\mathrm{cut}$ so that we include the entire relevant part in the first place.
The relevant metric is the order parameter's value at its maximum $\maxGap$.
In practice, we choose $x_\mathrm{cut} = 10$ for $\maxGap < \SI{4}{meV}$, $x_\mathrm{cut} = 20$ for $\maxGap < \SI{14}{meV}$, and $x_\mathrm{cut} = 25$ beyond that. 
These cutoffs are not sharp, i.e., varying these values slightly does not impact the results, as long as one chooses a large enough $N_k$.
In practice, $N_k = 30000$ produces good results for the latter case, while $N_k = 20000$ is sufficiently precise for the other two cases.

\subsection{Discussion of the numerical results}

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plots/gap_theta.pdf}

  The blue solid line shows the result for the full interaction \eqref{eqn:phonon_int}
  while the orange dashed line depicts the data for the approximation \eqref{eqn:approx_g}.

  \label{fig:gap_theta}

Let us begin by omitting the Coulomb interactions, i.e., setting $e=0$.
Then, the gap function naturally only includes the phononic contribution.
Often, the effective electron-electron interaction is assumed to be constant in a small region around the Fermi edge [einige Beispielzitate].
It is then described by
\begin{equation}
\label{eqn:approx_g}
  g(k, k') \coloneqq G \Theta(\debye - |\epsilon(k)|) \Theta(\debye - |\epsilon(k')|).
\end{equation}
We will briefly study this case as well to compare the results of both approaches.
Three exemplary gap functions for both, this approximate interaction and the full one \eqref{eqn:phonon_int}, are depicted in \autoref{fig:gap_theta}.
We set $N_k = 8000$ and $g=\{0.15, 0.5, 1.0\}$. The gap functions themselves are qualitatively generic.
Increasing $g$ increases their magnitude and increasing $\debye$ increases its width.
As to be expected, both functions are restricted to a small region around the Fermi edge.
The latter, however, is slightly wider but exhibits a smaller magnitude.
Its general form approaches the approximate form in the limit $g \ll 1$.
Usually, the focus of studies lies in the gap's magnitude at the Fermi edge.
Here, though the results differ slightly, the approximation \eqref{eqn:approx_g} is certainly sufficient for a qualitative description of the physics.
Nonetheless, we will continue using the form \eqref{eqn:phonon_int} for this article.

Next, we add the Coulomb interaction again which results in considerable changes to the gap function.
A plot of two exemplary gap functions can be seen in \autoref{fig:gap_coulomb}.
Here, we used $N_k = 20000$ and $g=0.8$. 
In the top panel, the screening is set to $\lambda = 10^{-4}$ while the bottom panel has it set to $\lambda = 1$.
Again, the functions are qualitatively generic with regard to change in $g$ and $\debye$.
Naturally, increasing the screening also increases the gap's magnitude.
Also, as mentioned before, the correction to the Fock energy $\epsilon_C$ is negligibly small.

A striking new feature is that the gap function, indepedent of the screening, stretches to all $k$ values.
While its main contribution is still confined to a small region around the edge, 
it approach a constant value for $k \to 0$ and follows a $1/k^2$ behavior as $k \to \infty$. 
A proof of this statement can be seen in appendix \ref{sec:limiting_gaps}.

Furthermore, due to the repulsive nature of the Coulomb interaction, the gap function has a radial node when said interaction starts dominating over the phononic one.

At this point, it should be mentioned that the complex phase of the gap function can still be fixed to be real.
We repeated the calculation allowing for an arbitrary complex phase, but the results are essentially identical:
Instead of changing sign at the nodal points, the complex phase of the gap function jumps by $\pi$.

Considering a small screening, i.e., $\lambda = 10^{-4}$ in panel (a), results in a rather narrow valley in $\deltaC$ around the Fermi edge.
It is noteworthy, that this result is essentially identical to the one obtained by omitting the screening entirely because this feature originates from integrating over a logarithmic singularity.
While this part of our calculations can still be easily performed without a screening, the later parts require at least a small one as to avoid numerically dividing by $0$.

The aforementioned feature disappears entirely if we use the screening wavevector presented in Ref. \cite{rickayzen1980}, i.e., $\lambda = 1$ in panel (b).
In this case, the contribution of $\deltaC$ around the Fermi edge is essentially a constant.
This corroborates the qualitative statements of the commonly used result by Morel and Anderson \cite{morel1962}
that a screened Coulomb interaction essentially boils down to a constant pseudopotential $\mu^*$ around the Fermi edge, making relevant interaction strength $g - \mu^*$ \cite{mielke1997,sigrist2005,kostrzewa2018,fischer2018,simonato2023}.
Bogoliubov provided a first expression \cite{bogoliubov1958} that in dimensionless form is given by
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:bogoliubov_mu}
  \mu = \frac{e^2 \rho_F }{4 \epsilon_0 \kf^2 } \ln \left( \frac{k_s^2 + 4 \kf^2}{k_s^2} \right).
\end{equation}
Later, Morel and Anderson showed that this expression takes a reduced value in the weak coupling limit $\mu \ll 1$ as
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:anderson_morel_mu}
  \mu^* = \frac{\mu}{1 + \mu \ln \left( \frac{\Ef}{\debye} \right)},
\end{equation}
which they finally used to obtain \cite{morel1962}
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:anderson_morel_gap}
  \ln \left( \frac{\maxGap}{2 \debye} \right) = -\frac{1}{g - \mu^*}.
\end{equation}
By means of this expression, we can fit our results for $\maxGap$. 
Note, however, that this expression was derived under the assumption of a constant phononic interaction.
We already saw in \autoref{fig:gap_theta} that our interaction leads to a slightly reduced gap value at the Fermi edge.
Thus, we introduce two additional fit parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ and fit to
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:fit_gap}
  \ln \left( \frac{\maxGap}{2 \debye} \right) = \ln \alpha - \frac{\beta}{g - \mu^*}.
\end{equation}
Here, $\alpha$ represents a constant ratio relating $\maxGap$ to $\debye$, while $\beta$ serves as the scaling factor in the exponential.
Naturally, based on \eqref{eqn:anderson_morel_gap}, we expect these values to be at least close to $1$.
In \autoref{fig:fit_gap_dependence}, we show the fits of the data both excluding and including the Coulomb interaction.
For the former, we investigated both the approximate \eqref{eqn:approx_g} and the full interaction \eqref{eqn:phonon_int},
and for the latter, we show only the full interaction and the screening is set to $\lambda=1$ and $\lambda=10^{-4}$.
The corresponding fit parameters are shown in \autoref{tab:fit_gap_dependence}.

First and foremost, since the approximation \eqref{eqn:approx_g} is just the standard BCS interaction, we expect exactly the BCS behavior $\ln(\maxGap / (2 \debye)) = -1/g$,
which is within numerical accuracy exactly what we find.
The full interaction \eqref{eqn:phonon_int} deviates from that only slightly, namely in $\alpha$, i.e., the ratio between $\maxGap$ and $\debye$ which is slightly smaller now.
Notably, the exponential scaling factor $\beta$ agrees well with the expectation $\beta=1$ in all cases.

Switching on the Coulomb interaction provides more insights.
Firstly, using a typical screening $\lambda=1$ results in a $\mu^*$ that is in reasonable agreement with \eqref{eqn:anderson_morel_mu}.
A small screening of $\lambda=10^{-4}$, however, yields considerable deviations.
Nevertheless, this is no contradiction to the original statement as it was derived for $\mu \ll 1$, which is no longer given here.

Lastly, it should be mentioned that the quantity $\maxGap / \debye$ is fully indepedent of $\debye$, i.e., $\maxGap \propto \debye$, and is therefore not explicitly depicted here.

As mentioned above, if we assume a constant interaction strength and neglect the Coulomb interaction,
we obtain constant order parameter $\Delta (k)$ around the Fermi edge.
It is then trivial to see that the quasiparticle dispersion
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:quasiparticle_energy}
  E(k) = \sqrt{ (\epsilon(k) + \epsilon_\mathrm{C}(k))^2 + \Delta^2 (k) }
\end{equation}
has its global minimum at the $\kf$.
However, this is not necessarily the case if the order parameter varies.
In principle, it could diminish faster than the single-particle dispersion $\epsilon(k)$ rises for $k\neq \kf$.
We can, in fact, observe this behavior if $g$ is large enough.
A plot of this can be seen in \autoref{fig:max_true}. In the upper panels (a-c), we show the quasiparticle energy \eqref{eqn:quasiparticle_energy} relative to $\maxGap$ for various $g$.
The Coulomb interaction is excluded in panel (a), and included with a screening of (b) $\lambda=1$ and (c) $\lambda=10^{-4}$.
For all cases, there exists a $g$, at which the dispersion's minimum moves away from $\kf$, though the shift in momentum is tiny.
We define the energy at this minimum as $\trueGap$ and compare it to $\maxGap$ in panel (d) of \autoref{fig:max_true}.
Obviously, the line begins at $\trueGap / \maxGap - 1 = 0$ as they are identical for moderate $g$.
Nevertheless, the above ratio slows down and even reverses as $g$ is further increased.

\autoref{fig:gap_behavior} shows the order parameter's peak value $\maxGap$ depending on the screening $\lambda$.
Naturally, it grows as the screening is increased, but it does not seem to follow any elementary function.
Expectedly, the function approaches a constant value for both cases $\lambda \to 0$ and $\lambda \to \infty$.
The general behavior is generic across various values of the phononic coupling strength $g$.

Analyzing collective excitations is more involved and lies beyond the simple mean-field approach discussed in the previous section.
Namely, we follow the iEoM approach as discussed in Ref. \cite{althuser2024} to compute the relevant Green's functions.
The occurring commutators are computed with respect to the full Hamiltonian \eqref{eqn:base_hamiltonian}, but the expectation values are computed on the mean-field level.


\begin{equation}
  B_k \coloneqq \frac{1}{\sqrt{V}} \sum_{\vec{q}} h(k - |\vec{q}|) O_{\vec{q}}.
\end{equation}
$O_{\vec{q}}$ represents $f_{\vec{q}}$ and $n_{\vec{k}\up} +  n_{-\vec{k}\down}$

\textbullet We will investigate the Higgs and the phase modes, given by
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
    A_\mathrm{Higgs} &\coloneqq \frac{1}{\sqrt{V}} \sum_{\vec{k}} \left( f_{\vec{k}}^\dagger + f_{\vec{k}} \right), \\
    A_\mathrm{Phase} &\coloneqq \frac{i}{\sqrt{V}} \sum_{\vec{k}} \left( f_{\vec{k}}^\dagger - f_{\vec{k}} \right).
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

\textbullet Note, that
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
    \Delta k \sum_{i=1}^{N_k} B_k &= \frac{\Delta k}{\sqrt{V}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_k} \sum_{\vec{q}} h(k - |\vec{q}|) O_{\vec{q}} \\
        &\approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{V}} \int \mathrm{d} k \sum_{\vec{q}} \delta(k - |\vec{q}|) O_{\vec{q}} \\
        &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{V}} \sum_{\vec{q}} O_{\vec{q}}.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
This indicates that the proper starting state $\vec{a}$ for, e.g., the Higgs mode is given by
\begin{equation}
    \vec{a}_i \coloneqq \begin{cases}
        1 & i < N_k \\ 0 & \mathrm{otherwise}
    \end{cases}.
\end{equation}

\textbullet For these calculations, we finally require at least a small screening because the commutators yield otherwise divergent terms.
As an example consider 
$\expec{[ f_{\vec{l}}^\dagger  - f_{\vec{l}} , [H,  f_{\vec{k}} - f_{\vec{k}}^\dagger ]]}$.
One of the resulting terms is
$V( 0 )  \langle f_{ \vec{l} } \rangle \langle f_{ \vec{k} } \rangle$,
where $V(0)$ refers to the Coulomb potential evaluated at $0$.
Because these terms are contributions to individual matrix elements of matrices that we need to manipulated in a non-trivial manner,
we cannot lift nor easily avoid these kinds of singularities and ultimately require a finite screening.

\subsection{Discussion of the numerical results}

\textbullet Cut the ieom basis so that it only uses the fine inner mesh, using $N_k=20000$.
Not necessary for the approximate interaction \eqref{eqn:approx_g} as the cutoff comes naturally with the Heaviside functions.

\textbullet No Coulomb interaction: extra modes as $g$ is increased - but only for rather large gaps \autoref{fig:resolvent_overview}.

\textbullet With Coulomb interaction: still extra modes for large $g$. No phase mode at 0 in accordance with the Anderson-Higgs mechanism \autoref{fig:resolvent_overview_coulomb}.

\textbullet \autoref{fig:heatmap_resolvent}: evolution of modes as $g$ is varied. (a) $\spectral{Higgs}$, (b) $\spectral{Phase}$.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item No Coulomb interaction: Phase mode at 0, multiple additional modes below the continuum.
  \item $\lambda=1$, i.e., weak Coulomb interaction: Phase mode not at $0$, but still below the continuum.
  Level repulsion around $g\approx 2.5$ with one of the additional modes. Lowest mode, for both $\spectral{Higgs}$ and $\spectral{Phase}$ is concave.
  \item $\lambda=10^{-4}$, i.e., strong/normal Coulomb interaction: Low lying phase mode is absent, yet additional modes appear at large $g$.
  Lowest modes are still concave.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Limiting behavior of the gap function}
\label{sec:limiting_gaps}
In this section, we want to briefly and analytically show how the gap function behaves in the limits $k \to 0$ and $k \to \infty$.
First and foremost, we take note that the phononic contribution \eqref{eqn:phonon_int} vanishes in both of these limits.
Thus, we will only discuss the Coulomb contribution \eqref{eqn:delta_coulomb}.
For the first case, consider
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  \lim_{k \to 0}& \deltaC (k) = \\
    &= \lim_{k \to 0} \frac{e^2}{8 \pi^2 \epsilon_0 k} \int_0^\infty \mathrm{d} q \expec{f_{q}^\dagger} q \ln \left( \frac{k_s^2 + (q + k)^2}{k_s^2 + (q - k)^2} \right)\nonumber \\
    &= \frac{e^2}{8 \pi^2 \epsilon_0} \lim_{k \to 0} \frac{1}{k} \int_0^\infty \mathrm{d} q \expec{f_{q}^\dagger} q \left( \frac{4 k q}{k_s^2 + q^2} + \mathcal{O}(k^3) \right) \\
    &= \frac{e^2}{2 \pi^2 \epsilon_0} \int_0^\infty \mathrm{d} q \expec{f_{q}^\dagger} \frac{q^2}{k_s^2 + q^2} \\
    &= \mathrm{const}.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
Similarly, we obtain for the second case
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  \deltaC (k \gg \kf ) &= \frac{e^2}{8 \pi^2 \epsilon_0 k} \int_0^\infty \mathrm{d} q \expec{f_{q}^\dagger} q \left( \frac{4q}{k} + \mathcal{O}\left( \frac{1}{k^3} \right) \right) \\
  &= \frac{e^2}{2 \pi^2 \epsilon_0 } \frac{1}{k^2} \int_0^\infty \mathrm{d} q q^2 \expec{f_{q}^\dagger} \\
  &\propto \frac{1}{k^2}.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
